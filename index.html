Background

Pregnancy risk apps are useful only if they measure the right signals.

Digital maternal health tools are becoming more common, but not every app uses the same health inputs, explains risk clearly, or prioritizes clinically meaningful indicators. Our research focuses on how apps classify pregnancy risk and whether they account for indicators linked to high-risk outcomes.

01

Maternal risk is multifactorial

Pregnancy risk can be shaped by blood sugar, gestational diabetes, blood pressure, BMI, age, medical history, and mental health factors.

02

Apps vary in quality

Some apps ask meaningful health questions, while others rely on generic wellness content that may not support risk awareness.

03

Benchmarks create accountability

A rubric makes it easier to compare apps fairly and identify which tools align best with data-backed pregnancy risk factors.

Problem statement

Pregnancy apps may claim risk support without measuring the strongest indicators.

If an app labels users as high risk or low risk but ignores major indicators like blood sugar and gestational diabetes, its risk guidance may be incomplete. The problem is not that apps exist. The problem is that users and providers need a way to judge whether those apps are actually using meaningful risk signals.

Mission, vision, impact

Turn model results into a practical app-grading standard.

Mission: Create a rubric that evaluates how well pregnancy health apps identify and communicate high-risk and low-risk pregnancy factors.
Vision: Help patients, providers, and designers recognize which apps are data-aware, transparent, and useful for maternal health decision support.
Impact: Make app evaluation clearer by comparing app features against the strongest indicators found in our data science analysis.

Data science

Our dataset becomes the benchmark for grading apps.

We used the Maternal Health Risk Assessment Dataset from Mendeley Data (Mojumdar et al., 2024) to identify which variables were most useful for predicting pregnancy risk. The cleaned dataset and model results shaped the rubric criteria.

Feature importance

Blood sugar and gestational diabetes drive the rubric.

Our analysis showed that Blood Sugar and Gestational Diabetes were the strongest indicators for distinguishing high-risk and low-risk pregnancy categories. That means apps should be scored heavily on whether they collect, explain, and respond to those factors.

So what?

The analysis gives us a way to judge app quality.

Insight 1: Apps that ignore blood sugar or gestational diabetes should lose major rubric points because those were the strongest risk indicators in our benchmark.
Insight 2: A good app should not only ask for health inputs; it should explain why those inputs matter for risk classification.
Insight 3: The best apps should be transparent about limitations and should encourage users to follow up with medical professionals.
Insight 4: Our work shifts the project from prediction alone to evaluation: we are grading how responsibly apps use pregnancy risk data.

Proposed solution

A rubric for grading pregnancy risk prediction apps.

We will evaluate four existing apps using our dataset as the benchmark for what a strong risk-aware app should consider. The rubric will score whether each app captures high-impact indicators, communicates risk clearly, and supports responsible next steps.

Conclusion

A good maternal health app should match the evidence.

Our project uses data science to move beyond general opinions about pregnancy apps. By turning model insights into a rubric, we can evaluate whether apps meaningfully address the indicators most connected to high-risk and low-risk pregnancy classification.

Limitations

What our project cannot prove yet.

Dataset scope: The benchmark depends on one dataset, so results may not represent every population or clinical setting.
App access: Some apps may hide features behind accounts, subscriptions, or region-specific settings.
Medical validation: Our rubric evaluates app quality, but it does not certify an app as clinically safe or medically approved.

Future considerations

Next steps for a stronger benchmark.

Evaluate more apps

Expand beyond four apps to see whether patterns hold across the pregnancy health app market.

Add expert review

Test usability
